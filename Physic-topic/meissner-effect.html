The Meissner Effect

Why do objects suddenly float above a magnet when cooled below a critical temperature? The answer lies deep inside quantum mechanics.

Temperature: 300 K | Normal conductor
01 — Motivation

Why Does Levitation Even Happen?

In 1933, German physicists Walther Meissner and Robert Ochsenfeld discovered something shocking: a superconductor doesn't just conduct electricity without resistance — it actively expels all magnetic fields from its interior.

Real World Use

🚄 Maglev Trains

Japan's SCMaglev reaches 603 km/h using superconducting coils that levitate the train via the Meissner effect — zero contact, zero friction.

Real World Use

🏥 MRI Scanners

The strong, stable magnetic fields inside MRI machines are generated by superconducting coils cooled with liquid helium at ~4 K.

Future Tech

⚡ Power Grids

Superconducting power cables could transmit electricity with zero loss, potentially revolutionizing global energy infrastructure.

🔑
Key Insight: Ordinary magnets attract or repel other magnets. But a superconductor actively "pushes back" against any magnetic field trying to enter it — this is what causes levitation.
02 — Core Concept

What's Actually Happening Inside?

🌡️ Above Critical Temperature (Normal State)

Electrons move through the metal independently, colliding with atoms and generating heat. Magnetic field lines pass freely through the material, just like any ordinary conductor.

Resistance is present. No levitation. Boring physics.

❄️ Below Critical Temperature (Superconducting State)

Electrons pair up into Cooper pairs — a quantum phenomenon where two electrons with opposite momenta and spins form a bound state.

These pairs condense into a single quantum state (Bose-Einstein condensate), moving without scattering. Zero resistance. And crucially — zero magnetic field inside.

⚛️
Cooper Pairs (BCS Theory, 1957): Two electrons that would normally repel each other can form a bound pair by interacting through phonons (lattice vibrations). These pairs obey Bose–Einstein statistics and can flow together without resistance.

How Does the Field Get Expelled?

When a superconductor cools below its critical temperature Tc, surface currents spontaneously appear. These persistent surface currents generate their own magnetic field that exactly cancels any external field inside the material.

This is described by the London equations — the magnetic field decays exponentially from the surface over a distance called the London penetration depth (λL).

B(x) = B₀ · e−x / λL
⚠️
Meissner ≠ Lenz's Law! Perfect conductors would also expel flux — but only if cooled in zero field. Superconductors expel flux even if cooled inside a magnetic field. This is the true Meissner effect, and it proves superconductivity is a distinct thermodynamic state.

05 — Phase Diagram

Type I vs Type II Superconductors

Not all superconductors behave the same. There are two types, defined by how they respond to increasing magnetic field strength.

Type I

Complete Expulsion

Below Hc: full Meissner state — perfect diamagnet, B=0 inside.

Above Hc: abrupt transition to normal state. No intermediate.

Examples: Lead, Mercury, Aluminum, Tin.

Type II

Mixed State (Vortex Phase)

Between Hc1 and Hc2: field penetrates via quantized vortex tubes — but current still flows without resistance.

Examples: YBCO, Niobium, MgB₂ — used in real applications.

🔬
Flux Pinning: In Type II superconductors, impurities and defects can "pin" vortices in place. This allows levitation at multiple angles, including the famous locked-in-place demonstration where a superconductor hovers frozen in position.
06 — Key Takeaways

What You Should Remember

🌡️
Critical Temperature Tc The exact threshold below which a material becomes superconducting. Varies by material (4 K for mercury, 93 K for YBCO).
🧲
Perfect Diamagnetism Superconductors have magnetic susceptibility χ = −1. They perfectly repel external B fields from their interior.
⚡
Surface Currents Persistent currents spontaneously form on the surface to generate an opposing field — they require no energy source.
👫
Cooper Pairs (BCS) Electrons pair via phonon interaction. Pairs condense into a macroscopic quantum state — the origin of zero resistance.
🔄
Thermodynamic State Unlike Lenz's law, the Meissner effect works even if the field was applied before cooling — proving it's a true quantum phase transition.
